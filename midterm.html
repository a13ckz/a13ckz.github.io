Seatwork #1 — Operators

A timed in-class seatwork activity focused on Java operators. Submitted on paper within a strict one-hour window. The activity tested understanding of various Java operator types including arithmetic, relational, logical, bitwise, ternary, and assignment operators — requiring both theoretical knowledge and practical application.

↳ Reflection — Quizzes, Activities & Seatwork

When Sir Bedis first introduced the topics — operators, variables, control flow, and methods — it honestly felt like a lot to absorb all at once. The seatwork especially had this way of throwing everything at you in a compressed time window, which made it stressful at first. There were moments during the operator exercises where I second-guessed myself on things like bitwise operations or the exact behavior of pre- and post-increment in complex expressions. The ternary operator was surprisingly tricky once nested logic got involved.

But once Sir Bedis walked through the concepts in class — breaking down each operator group, showing live examples, and explaining the why behind each behavior — everything started clicking into place. His explanations were clear and direct, and he had a way of making abstract syntax feel practical and grounded. The activities stopped feeling like obstacles and started feeling like checkpoints I could actually pass. By the time we got to the midterm activities, there was a real sense of progress — the concepts that once felt foreign had become familiar tools.

↳ Reflection — Midterm Exam

Looking back on the midterm exam, I have to be honest with myself: I wasn't fully prepared, and that showed in how I approached it. The period leading up to the exam was a particularly demanding stretch — multiple subjects all converging at the same time, each with their own requirements and deadlines. Between juggling outputs, readings, and reviews for other courses, Object-Oriented Programming unfortunately ended up getting less of my attention than it deserved.

There were concepts I knew I understood but couldn't articulate under pressure, and there were parts of the exam where I felt the gaps from not having reviewed thoroughly enough. It wasn't a lack of interest or effort across the board — it was the very real constraint of limited time and energy spread too thin across too many commitments at once.

That said, I don't see this as a moment to make excuses — I see it as a lesson in prioritization and time management. OOP is a subject that rewards consistent practice, and I know now that cramming or last-minute reviewing isn't enough for something this layered. For the finals, I intend to be more deliberate: reviewing early, practicing code regularly, and not letting other subjects crowd this one out. This exam was a wake-up call, and I'm taking it seriously.
